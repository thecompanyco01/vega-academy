Trial Balance: The Complete Guide for Bookkeepers Moving Into Advisory

Updated March 2026 · 20 min read · 12,100 monthly searches

Bottom Line: A trial balance is a worksheet that lists all general ledger account balances at a specific point in time. If total debits equal total credits, your books are mathematically in balance. It's the foundation of accurate financial reporting — and a skill that separates compliance bookkeepers from advisory professionals.

What Is a Trial Balance?

A trial balance is an internal accounting report that lists every account in your general ledger along with its debit or credit balance. It serves as a checkpoint before preparing financial statements.

Think of it as a "proof" step. If debits don't equal credits, something went wrong in your journal entries — and you need to find it before producing financial statements for clients or stakeholders.

Why the Trial Balance Matters

Many bookkeepers treat the trial balance as a formality. Advisory-minded professionals see it differently:

  • Error detection: It catches transposition errors, omitted entries, and posting mistakes
  • Financial statement accuracy: Every balance sheet and income statement starts from the trial balance
  • Audit readiness: Auditors always request the trial balance first — a clean one signals competence
  • Client conversations: When you can walk a business owner through their trial balance, you're speaking the language of a CFO, not a data entry clerk

Types of Trial Balance

1. Unadjusted Trial Balance

Prepared after posting all journal entries for the period but before adjusting entries. It shows raw account balances and is the starting point for the adjustment process.

2. Adjusted Trial Balance

Prepared after recording adjusting entries (accruals, deferrals, depreciation, etc.). This is the version used to prepare financial statements. If you're providing advisory services, this is where you add the most value — identifying the adjustments that paint the true financial picture.

3. Post-Closing Trial Balance

Prepared after closing entries have zeroed out temporary accounts (revenue, expenses). Only permanent accounts (assets, liabilities, equity) remain. This confirms the books are ready for the next period.

How to Prepare a Trial Balance: Step by Step

Step 1: Complete All Journal Entries

Ensure every transaction for the period has been recorded and posted to the general ledger. Missing entries are the #1 cause of trial balance issues.

Step 2: List Every Account

Extract all accounts from the general ledger. Organize them in standard order: assets, liabilities, equity, revenue, expenses.

Step 3: Record Each Balance

For each account, record its ending balance in either the debit or credit column. Assets and expenses are normally debits; liabilities, equity, and revenue are normally credits.

Step 4: Total Both Columns

Add up all debits and all credits. If they're equal, your trial balance is in balance. If not, you have work to do.

Step 5: Investigate Differences

If the trial balance doesn't balance, the difference gives you clues. A difference divisible by 9 usually indicates a transposition error. A difference equal to twice an account balance suggests a debit/credit reversal.

Common Trial Balance Errors

  • Transposition errors: Writing $540 instead of $450. The difference is always divisible by 9.
  • Slide errors: Moving a decimal point (recording $1,000 as $100). The difference is divisible by 9.
  • Omission: Forgetting to post an entire journal entry. Both debit and credit sides stay equal, but balances are wrong.
  • Commission errors: Posting the right amount to the wrong account (e.g., debiting Supplies instead of Equipment).
  • Compensating errors: Two errors that cancel each other out. The trial balance balances, but individual accounts are wrong.

Advisory insight: When you find and explain these errors to clients, you're demonstrating the analytical thinking that justifies advisory-level fees — not just data entry corrections.

Trial Balance in Modern Accounting Software

QuickBooks, Xero, and most modern platforms generate trial balances automatically. So why does this knowledge matter?

  • You need to interpret it: Software generates the report; you need to spot what's wrong and what it means
  • Adjusting entries still require judgment: Software can't decide if revenue should be deferred or if depreciation estimates need changing
  • Client communication: You'll use the trial balance to explain financial health during advisory meetings
  • Conversion projects: When migrating clients between systems, the trial balance is your reconciliation anchor

From Trial Balance to Advisory: The Upgrade Path

Most bookkeepers stop at "does it balance?" Advisory professionals go further:

  1. Trend analysis: Compare trial balances across periods to identify unusual changes
  2. Ratio analysis: Calculate key ratios directly from trial balance figures (current ratio, debt-to-equity, profit margin)
  3. Budget variance: Compare actual trial balance figures to budgeted amounts and explain significant variances
  4. Forecasting: Use trial balance trends to project future financial positions

This is the difference between a $35/hour bookkeeper and a $150+/hour fractional CFO. Same data, different depth of analysis.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a trial balance in simple terms?

A trial balance is a list of all accounts in your general ledger with their debit or credit balances. If total debits equal total credits, your books are mathematically in balance.

What happens if a trial balance doesn't balance?

It means there's an error in your journal entries or posting. You need to investigate — check for transposition errors, omitted entries, or debit/credit reversals until you find the discrepancy.

Can a trial balance be wrong even if it balances?

Yes. Errors of omission (leaving out entire transactions), commission errors (posting to the wrong account), and compensating errors can all exist in a balanced trial balance. That's why the trial balance is a necessary but not sufficient check.

How often should you prepare a trial balance?

At minimum monthly, before preparing financial statements. Many firms prepare one after every adjusting entry batch. For advisory clients, quarterly deep-dive analysis of the trial balance is valuable.
