What Is a Balance Sheet?

A balance sheet (also called a statement of financial position) is one of three core financial statements. It provides a snapshot of what a company owns (assets), what it owes (liabilities), and the residual value belonging to owners (equity) at a specific date.

The Fundamental Equation: Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity. This equation must ALWAYS balance — hence the name "balance sheet."

Unlike the income statement (which covers a period), the balance sheet captures a moment in time. Think of it as a photograph of the company's financial health on a specific date.

How to Read a Balance Sheet: Key Ratios

Reading a balance sheet isn't just listing numbers — it's extracting insights. Here are the ratios every advisory professional should calculate:

1. Current Ratio (Liquidity)

Formula: Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities

Example: $133,000 ÷ $37,000 = 3.59

A ratio above 1.5 indicates strong short-term liquidity. ABC Consulting can comfortably meet its short-term obligations.

2. Debt-to-Equity Ratio (Leverage)

Formula: Total Liabilities ÷ Total Equity

Example: $65,000 ÷ $97,000 = 0.67

Under 1.0 means the company is primarily funded by equity rather than debt — a healthy position for a small business.

3. Working Capital

Formula: Current Assets − Current Liabilities

Example: $133,000 − $37,000 = $96,000

Positive working capital of $96,000 means the business has plenty of runway for operations.

4. Quick Ratio (Acid Test)

Formula: (Cash + Accounts Receivable) ÷ Current Liabilities

Example: ($85,000 + $42,000) ÷ $37,000 = 3.43

Excludes less-liquid assets like prepaid expenses. Still extremely healthy.

Balance Sheet vs. Income Statement vs. Cash Flow

StatementShowsTime FrameKey Insight
Balance SheetAssets, Liabilities, EquityPoint in timeFinancial position
Income StatementRevenue, Expenses, ProfitPeriodProfitability
Cash Flow StatementCash inflows/outflowsPeriodCash generation

As an advisor, you need all three statements to give clients a complete picture. The balance sheet alone can be misleading — a profitable company on the income statement can still be cash-poor.

Common Balance Sheet Mistakes

  • Misclassifying current vs. non-current: Debt due within 12 months must be current
  • Not reconciling accounts receivable: Stale AR inflates assets
  • Ignoring depreciation: Assets without accumulated depreciation overstate value
  • Missing accrued liabilities: Unpaid wages, taxes, and interest must be accrued
  • Owner draws vs. salary: Often confused in small business equity sections

How Advisory Professionals Use Balance Sheets

If you're a bookkeeper transitioning to a fractional CFO or advisory role, the balance sheet becomes your primary tool for client conversations:

  1. Cash planning: Use the current ratio and working capital trends to forecast cash needs
  2. Debt capacity: Analyze debt-to-equity to advise on loan applications
  3. Growth readiness: Compare AR growth to revenue growth — if AR grows faster, collections are slowing
  4. Exit planning: A clean, well-organized balance sheet increases business valuation
  5. Monthly reviews: Present balance sheet trends to clients as part of your monthly close package

Industry-Specific Balance Sheet Considerations

Service Businesses

Minimal inventory, heavy on accounts receivable. Watch for aging AR — service businesses often struggle with collections.

Retail/E-commerce

Inventory is the largest current asset. Monitor inventory turnover and watch for obsolescence.

Construction

Work-in-progress (WIP) and retainage make construction balance sheets unique. Under-billings and over-billings require special attention.

Nonprofits

Instead of equity, nonprofits report "net assets" — with or without donor restrictions. Nonprofit bookkeeping follows different presentation standards.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a balance sheet?

A balance sheet is a financial statement that reports a company's assets, liabilities, and shareholders' equity at a specific point in time. It follows the fundamental equation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity.

What are the 3 sections of a balance sheet?

The three sections are: Assets (what the company owns), Liabilities (what it owes), and Shareholders' Equity (the owner's residual interest). Assets are divided into current and non-current, as are liabilities.

How do you analyze a balance sheet?

Key analysis methods include: calculating the current ratio (current assets ÷ current liabilities), debt-to-equity ratio, working capital, and comparing trends over multiple periods. Advisory professionals use these ratios to give clients actionable financial insights.
