Inventory Accounting: The Complete Guide for 2026

Published March 8, 2026 · 14 min read · By Fractional CFO School

Inventory accounting is one of the most important — and most frequently botched — areas of small business bookkeeping. With 1,000 monthly searches and growing demand as ecommerce and product-based businesses proliferate, mastering inventory accounting positions you as an indispensable advisor. This guide covers everything from basic costing methods to advanced advisory strategies.

Why Inventory Accounting Matters

Inventory is often a company's single largest asset. Get the accounting wrong and you distort the balance sheet, misstate cost of goods sold (COGS), and produce unreliable profit margins. For product businesses, inventory accounting isn't an afterthought — it's the foundation of financial accuracy.

Poor inventory accounting leads to:

  • Phantom profits — Overstated margins because COGS is understated
  • Tax problems — Inventory errors cascade directly into taxable income
  • Cash flow blindness — Money tied up in unsold inventory doesn't show on the P&L
  • Bad purchasing decisions — Can't optimize what you can't measure

Inventory Costing Methods

FIFO (First In, First Out)

Under FIFO, the oldest inventory costs are assigned to COGS first. In periods of rising prices, FIFO produces lower COGS and higher reported profits. It's the most common method and aligns with how most businesses physically move inventory.

PurchaseUnitsCost/UnitTotal
January 1100$10.00$1,000
February 1100$12.00$1,200
March 1100$14.00$1,400

If you sell 150 units under FIFO: COGS = (100 × $10) + (50 × $12) = $1,600. Remaining inventory: (50 × $12) + (100 × $14) = $2,000.

LIFO (Last In, First Out)

Under LIFO, the newest (most expensive) inventory costs hit COGS first. This produces higher COGS and lower profits in inflationary periods — which means lower taxes. LIFO is only allowed under US GAAP, not IFRS.

Same example, 150 units sold under LIFO: COGS = (100 × $14) + (50 × $12) = $2,000. Remaining inventory: (50 × $12) + (100 × $10) = $1,600.

Advisory angle: The FIFO vs LIFO choice has real tax implications. Clients switching methods can save (or owe) significant taxes. This is a high-value advisory conversation.

Weighted Average Cost

Average all inventory costs together. Simple, consistent, and works well for businesses with many similar items (commodities, consumables). Using the same example: Average cost = $3,600 ÷ 300 = $12/unit. COGS for 150 units = $1,800.

Specific Identification

Track the actual cost of each individual item. Required for high-value, unique goods (cars, jewelry, custom equipment). Impractical for high-volume businesses but essential for luxury and specialty retail.

Inventory Valuation: Lower of Cost or Market

Under both GAAP and IFRS, inventory must be reported at the lower of cost or market value (LCM / NRV). This means if inventory has lost value — due to obsolescence, damage, or market changes — you must write it down.

Common write-down scenarios:

  • Seasonal merchandise — Holiday inventory still on shelves in February
  • Technology products — Last year's model at full cost when the new model launched
  • Perishable goods — Food, cosmetics, or pharmaceuticals approaching expiration
  • Damaged goods — Items that can only be sold at a discount

Industry-Specific Inventory Challenges

Ecommerce / Amazon Sellers

Multi-channel selling (Amazon, Shopify, eBay, wholesale) means inventory is physically in multiple locations. FBA fees, returns processing, and marketplace-specific accounting rules add complexity. The bookkeeper who can reconcile Amazon settlement reports with QuickBooks is worth their weight in gold.

Manufacturing

Raw materials → work-in-process (WIP) → finished goods. Three stages of inventory, each with different valuation rules. Job costing vs. process costing. Overhead allocation. This is advanced territory — and premium-priced.

Restaurants & Food Service

Perishable inventory, high waste rates, and food cost percentages that make or break profitability. A restaurant's target food cost is typically 28-35% of revenue. Tracking actual vs. theoretical food cost reveals theft, waste, and portioning problems.

Retail

High SKU counts, seasonal fluctuations, markdowns, and shrinkage (theft + damage + admin errors). The retail inventory method estimates ending inventory based on the cost-to-retail ratio — essential for businesses with thousands of SKUs.

Common Inventory Accounting Mistakes

  1. Not reconciling physical counts to book records — Shrinkage and errors compound over time
  2. Inconsistent costing methods — Switching between FIFO and average without proper accounting treatment
  3. Ignoring obsolescence — Carrying dead stock at full cost inflates the balance sheet
  4. Mixing inventory with expenses — Buying inventory and expensing it immediately (common in QuickBooks)
  5. No cutoff procedures — Goods received but not invoiced (or vice versa) at period end
