Bank Reconciliation: Complete Guide to Reconciling Your Books

Updated March 2026 · 18 min read · 8,100 monthly searches

Bottom Line: Bank reconciliation is the process of matching your accounting records to your bank statement to ensure they agree. It's the single most important internal control for any business — catching errors, preventing fraud, and ensuring your financial reports are accurate.

What Is Bank Reconciliation?

Bank reconciliation compares two sets of records:

  1. Your books (the cash balance in your accounting software)
  2. The bank statement (what the bank says you have)

These two numbers almost never match exactly. The reconciliation process identifies and accounts for every difference until both sides agree.

Why Bank Reconciliation Matters

  • Catches errors: Typos in amounts, duplicate entries, missed transactions
  • Detects fraud: Unauthorized transactions, forged checks, embezzlement
  • Ensures accuracy: Your financial reports are only as good as your bank reconciliation
  • Cash flow management: Know your true cash position, not what you think it is
  • Audit readiness: Clean reconciliations are the first thing auditors check
  • Tax accuracy: Prevents reporting incorrect income or expenses

Step-by-Step Bank Reconciliation Process

Step 1: Get Your Materials

  • Bank statement for the period (monthly is standard)
  • Your cash account register from QuickBooks/Xero
  • Previous month's reconciliation (for reference)

Step 2: Compare Ending Balances

Note the ending balance on the bank statement and the ending balance in your books. The difference is what you need to reconcile.

Step 3: Identify Outstanding Items

ItemEffectAction
Outstanding checksIn your books, not on bank statementSubtract from bank balance
Deposits in transitIn your books, not on bank statementAdd to bank balance
Bank fees/chargesOn bank statement, not in your booksRecord in your books
Interest earnedOn bank statement, not in your booksRecord in your books
NSF/bounced checksBank reversed a depositReverse in your books
ErrorsEither sideCorrect where the error occurred

Step 4: Adjust Your Books

Record any transactions that appear on the bank statement but not in your books:

  • Bank service charges → Debit Bank Fees Expense, Credit Cash
  • Interest income → Debit Cash, Credit Interest Income
  • NSF checks → Debit Accounts Receivable, Credit Cash
  • Automatic payments you forgot to record

Step 5: Verify the Reconciliation

Bank Statement Balance

+ Deposits in transit

- Outstanding checks

± Bank errors

= Adjusted Bank Balance


Book Balance

+ Interest earned

- Bank fees

- NSF checks

± Book errors

= Adjusted Book Balance


✅ Adjusted Bank Balance MUST equal Adjusted Book Balance

Bank Reconciliation in QuickBooks Online

QuickBooks makes reconciliation straightforward:

  1. Go to Settings → Reconcile
  2. Select the bank account
  3. Enter the ending balance and date from your bank statement
  4. Check off each transaction that appears on both the bank statement and QBO
  5. The "Difference" field should reach $0.00
  6. Click "Finish Now" when balanced

Pro tip: Use the bank feed feature to auto-match transactions, but always verify — auto-matching isn't perfect.

Common Reconciliation Problems & Solutions

Problem: The difference is a round number

Solution: Look for a missing transaction of exactly that amount. Round differences usually mean something wasn't recorded.

Problem: The difference is exactly double a transaction

Solution: The transaction was recorded on the wrong side (debit vs. credit) or duplicated.

Problem: The difference is small and odd

Solution: Look for transposition errors (e.g., $1,234 entered as $1,243 — difference of $9, which is always divisible by 9).

Problem: Dozens of unmatched transactions

Solution: The client may not be recording transactions regularly. Set up bank feeds and establish a weekly bookkeeping routine.

Best Practices for Bank Reconciliation

  • Reconcile monthly — at minimum. Weekly is better for high-volume businesses.
  • Don't let it pile up. A 3-month backlog takes 10x longer than three monthly reconciliations.
  • Separate duties. The person who writes checks shouldn't reconcile the bank account.
  • Keep documentation. Save the bank statement and reconciliation report for each period.
  • Investigate old outstanding items. A check outstanding for 6+ months needs attention — void and reissue or write it off.
  • Reconcile all accounts. Don't forget credit cards, PayPal, Stripe, and other payment platforms.

The Advisory Angle: Turning Reconciliation Into Revenue

Bank reconciliation is where many bookkeeper-client relationships start. But it's also where advisory relationships are born:

  • Spot cash flow patterns: "Your cash balance drops 40% every quarter — let's build a cash reserve strategy."
  • Identify unnecessary expenses: "You're paying $847/month in subscriptions you might not need."
  • Catch revenue leaks: "Three invoices over $5,000 haven't been collected in 90+ days."
  • Prevent fraud: "There are ATM withdrawals from a location your business doesn't operate in."

A bookkeeper who just reconciles charges $30-50/hour. A bookkeeper who reconciles AND provides these insights charges $100-200/hour. Same process, different value delivery.
