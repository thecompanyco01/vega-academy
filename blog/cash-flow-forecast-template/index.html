What Is a Cash Flow Forecast?

A cash flow forecast is a projection of your expected cash inflows and outflows over a future period. Unlike a profit and loss statement (which includes non-cash items like depreciation), a cash flow forecast focuses exclusively on when actual cash moves in and out of your bank account.

This distinction matters because profitable companies go bankrupt all the time — not because they lack revenue, but because they run out of cash. A company with $500K in accounts receivable and $0 in the bank can't make payroll, regardless of how "profitable" the P&L says they are.

The 13-Week Cash Flow Forecast: Your Most Important Tool

The 13-week cash flow forecast is the gold standard for short-term cash management. Here's why CFOs and fractional advisors prefer this timeframe:

  • 13 weeks ≈ one quarter — aligns with business planning cycles
  • Weekly granularity — catches cash crunches that monthly views miss
  • Rolling update — each week, drop the oldest week, add a new one at the end
  • Actionable — specific enough to inform decisions like "can we hire this month?"

The key insight: base inflows on when cash actually arrives, not when you invoice. If your average collection period is 35 days, a $50K invoice sent this week won't hit your bank for 5 weeks. The 13-week forecast makes this visible.

12-Month Cash Flow Projection

While the 13-week forecast manages short-term cash, the 12-month projection supports strategic planning:

  • Annual budgeting — ties operating budget to cash reality
  • Seasonal planning — identifies cash-tight months before they arrive
  • Bank/investor presentations — required for most credit applications and fundraising
  • Hiring decisions — shows whether you can sustain additional headcount

Scenario Analysis: Best, Expected, and Worst Case

Every serious cash flow forecast should include scenario modeling. What happens if:

  • Your biggest client pays 30 days late?
  • Revenue drops 20% next quarter?
  • You land that big contract you've been chasing?
  • A key employee leaves and you need to hire a replacement?

Scenario analysis turns a cash flow forecast from a prediction into a decision tool. Present all three scenarios to clients — it demonstrates sophistication and builds trust. This is how fractional CFOs deliver strategic value beyond basic bookkeeping.

How to Build a Cash Flow Forecast in Excel

Step-by-step process:

  1. Start with your current bank balance — this is your beginning cash position
  2. Map out known cash inflows — contracts with specific payment dates, recurring revenue, known collections
  3. Estimate variable inflows — use historical collection rates to project AR collections
  4. List all cash outflows by week — payroll dates, rent due dates, vendor payment terms, tax deadlines
  5. Calculate net cash flow per week — inflows minus outflows
  6. Calculate ending balance — beginning balance + net cash flow = ending balance (which becomes next week's beginning balance)
  7. Set minimum cash thresholds — highlight any week where cash drops below your safety buffer
  8. Update weekly with actuals — replace projections with actual numbers, re-forecast remaining weeks

Cash Flow Forecasting Tips for Advisors

  • Update before every client meeting — stale forecasts destroy credibility
  • Track forecast accuracy — compare projections to actuals. Under 10% variance = good. Over 25% = model needs work
  • Build in a cash buffer — most businesses should maintain 2-3 months of operating expenses as reserve
  • Separate operating from financing — loan proceeds inflate cash but aren't revenue
  • Focus on collections, not revenue — revenue recognition ≠ cash received
  • Present visually — charts showing the ending balance trend are more impactful than spreadsheet numbers

Frequently Asked Questions

What should a cash flow forecast template include?

Beginning cash balance, all cash inflows (collections, other revenue), all cash outflows (payroll, rent, vendors, taxes, debt), net cash flow, and ending cash balance. The best templates include 13-week rolling + 12-month views + scenario analysis.

How often should I update a cash flow forecast?

Weekly for the 13-week forecast (update with actuals, re-project remaining weeks). Monthly for the 12-month projection. More frequently during cash-tight periods.

What is a 13-week cash flow forecast?

A projection of cash inflows and outflows week by week for one quarter. The gold standard for short-term cash management because it catches problems monthly views miss.

Can I use Excel for cash flow forecasting?

Yes — Excel is still the most widely used tool. It's flexible, free, and familiar. Purpose-built FP&A software is only needed when spreadsheet complexity becomes unmanageable.
