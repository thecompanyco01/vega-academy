What Is Internal Audit?

Internal audit is an independent, objective assurance and consulting activity designed to add value and improve an organization's operations. It helps organizations accomplish their objectives by bringing a systematic, disciplined approach to evaluate and improve the effectiveness of risk management, control, and governance processes.

Unlike external audits (which verify financial statements for stakeholders), internal audits focus on operational efficiency, risk management, compliance, and internal controls. Internal auditors are embedded within organizations and serve as trusted advisors to management and the board.

The Internal Audit Process

1

Risk Assessment & Annual Planning

Identify and prioritize key risks across the organization. Develop an annual audit plan based on risk ratings, management input, and regulatory requirements.

2

Engagement Planning

Define audit objectives, scope, and timeline. Gather background information, review prior audit findings, and prepare the audit program.

3

Fieldwork & Testing

Execute audit procedures — test controls, sample transactions, interview process owners, analyze data. Document findings with evidence.

4

Reporting

Draft audit report with findings, root causes, and recommendations. Present to management and audit committee. Rate findings by severity.

5

Follow-Up

Track remediation of audit findings. Verify management has implemented agreed-upon corrective actions. Report on open items.

Internal Audit Frameworks

IIA Standards (International Professional Practices Framework)

The Institute of Internal Auditors (IIA) sets the global standards for internal audit practice. The IPPF includes:

  • Mission of Internal Audit — Enhance and protect organizational value
  • Core Principles — Integrity, competence, objectivity, alignment with strategy
  • Code of Ethics — Professional standards of conduct
  • International Standards — Attribute and performance standards

COSO Internal Control Framework

The Committee of Sponsoring Organizations (COSO) framework is the most widely used internal control framework globally. It covers five components:

  1. Control Environment
  2. Risk Assessment
  3. Control Activities
  4. Information & Communication
  5. Monitoring Activities

Three Lines Model

Updated from the "Three Lines of Defense" model, this IIA framework clarifies roles:

  • First Line: Management (owns and manages risk)
  • Second Line: Risk & compliance functions (oversight)
  • Third Line: Internal audit (independent assurance)

Key Certifications

  • CIA (Certified Internal Auditor) — The most recognized internal audit certification globally. Three-part exam covering internal audit essentials, practice, and business knowledge.
  • CISA (Certified Information Systems Auditor) — Focused on IT audit. Highly valued as technology risk grows.
  • CFE (Certified Fraud Examiner) — Valuable for auditors focused on fraud prevention and detection.
  • CPA (Certified Public Accountant) — Broadens career options across audit disciplines.
Career Transition from Bookkeeping: Bookkeepers already understand transaction flows, reconciliation, and financial records — core competencies for internal audit. Consider starting with a CIA certification while gaining experience in compliance or quality assurance roles.

Technology in Internal Audit

Modern internal audit heavily leverages technology:

  • Data Analytics: Tools like ACL, IDEA, and Tableau for continuous auditing
  • Audit Management Software: TeamMate+, AuditBoard, Workiva for workflow management
  • Robotic Process Automation (RPA): Automating repetitive audit procedures
  • AI/Machine Learning: Anomaly detection, predictive risk assessment
  • GRC Platforms: Integrated governance, risk, and compliance management

Building an Internal Audit Career

Internal audit is one of the best career paths for accounting professionals who want variety, strategic influence, and strong earning potential. Unlike external audit, you get deep exposure to business operations across the entire organization.

Many CFOs, COOs, and board members started their careers in internal audit. The skills you develop — risk assessment, process improvement, stakeholder management — are directly transferable to executive leadership.
