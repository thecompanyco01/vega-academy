Variance Analysis: The Advisory Skill That Turns Budgets Into Profit

Updated March 2026 · 18 min read · 2,900 monthly searches

Bottom Line: Variance analysis compares actual financial results to budgeted or expected amounts, then investigates the reasons for differences. It's the bridge between "we made a budget" and "we're actually using it to make better decisions." For bookkeepers building advisory practices, this is one of the most in-demand skills CFOs and controllers use daily.

What Is Variance Analysis?

Variance analysis is the process of comparing planned (budgeted) financial outcomes to actual results, then analyzing the differences (variances) to understand why they occurred and what to do about them.

Variance = Actual Amount − Budgeted Amount

A positive revenue variance means you earned more than planned (good). A positive expense variance means you spent more than planned (bad). Context determines whether a variance is favorable or unfavorable.

Favorable vs. Unfavorable Variances

Type Revenue Expenses
Favorable (F)Actual > BudgetActual < Budget
Unfavorable (U)Actual < BudgetActual > Budget

Important: "Favorable" doesn't always mean "good." If marketing spend is favorable (under budget), it might mean you didn't execute your marketing plan — which could hurt future revenue. Always investigate the why behind the number.

Types of Variance Analysis

1. Revenue Variance

Breaks down revenue differences into price and volume components:

  • Price variance: (Actual Price − Budgeted Price) × Actual Volume
  • Volume variance: (Actual Volume − Budgeted Volume) × Budgeted Price

Example: You budgeted 100 units at $50 = $5,000. Actual: 90 units at $55 = $4,950.

  • Price variance: ($55 − $50) × 90 = +$450 (F)
  • Volume variance: (90 − 100) × $50 = -$500 (U)
  • Total variance: -$50 (U)

The business raised prices (good) but sold fewer units (bad). Net result: slightly under budget. This nuance is lost without variance decomposition.

2. Cost/Expense Variance

Similarly decomposed into price and efficiency components:

  • Rate variance: (Actual Rate − Standard Rate) × Actual Hours
  • Efficiency variance: (Actual Hours − Standard Hours) × Standard Rate

3. Profit Variance

The overall difference between actual and budgeted profit. This is the summary number — but always drill down into revenue and cost variances to understand the drivers.

4. Static vs. Flexible Budget Variance

  • Static budget variance: Compares actuals to the original budget (fixed at beginning of period)
  • Flexible budget variance: Adjusts the budget for actual volume, then compares. This isolates operational performance from volume changes.

Flexible budget variance is more meaningful for advisory conversations because it answers: "Given the volume we actually achieved, how well did we control costs?"

Setting Materiality Thresholds

Not every variance needs investigation. Set thresholds:

  • Percentage-based: Investigate variances > 10% (common starting point)
  • Dollar-based: Investigate variances > $5,000 (adjust for business size)
  • Combined: Investigate if > 10% AND > $2,000 (avoids chasing small amounts that happen to have large percentage swings)

Root Cause Investigation Framework

When you find a significant variance, investigate systematically:

  1. Timing: Is this a timing difference that will self-correct? (e.g., an invoice came in early)
  2. Volume: Did we sell more/less than expected?
  3. Price: Did pricing change (on revenue or cost side)?
  4. Efficiency: Did we use more resources than planned?
  5. External: Market changes, supplier price changes, economic shifts?
  6. Budget accuracy: Was the budget realistic in the first place?

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should variance analysis be performed?

Monthly for management reporting. Weekly for key metrics (revenue, cash). Quarterly for deep-dive strategic analysis. The frequency depends on the client's decision-making cadence.

What if the budget itself was wrong?

This is common and important to acknowledge. If the budget was unrealistic, the variance isn't operational — it's a planning issue. Use this insight to improve next period's budget. Some companies use rolling forecasts instead of static annual budgets for this reason.

What's the difference between variance analysis and trend analysis?

Variance analysis compares actual to plan (budget). Trend analysis compares actual to prior periods (month-over-month, year-over-year). Both are valuable. Variance analysis asks "did we hit our targets?" Trend analysis asks "are things getting better or worse?"
