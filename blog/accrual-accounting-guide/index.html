Accrual Accounting: What It Is, How It Works, and Why It Matters

Updated March 2026 · 20 min read · 9,900 monthly searches

Bottom Line: Accrual accounting records revenue when earned and expenses when incurred — regardless of when cash changes hands. It's the standard under GAAP, required for larger businesses, and the foundation of accurate financial reporting. For bookkeepers, mastering accrual accounting is the gateway to high-value advisory work.

What Is Accrual Accounting?

Accrual accounting is a method that records financial transactions when they occur, not when cash is exchanged. It follows two fundamental principles:

  1. Revenue Recognition Principle: Record revenue when it's earned (when you deliver the service or product), not when payment is received
  2. Matching Principle: Record expenses in the same period as the revenue they helped generate

These two principles create a more accurate picture of a business's financial performance than cash-based accounting.

How Accrual Accounting Works in Practice

Revenue Recognition Example

A consulting firm completes a $15,000 project in February but invoices the client on March 1st and receives payment on April 15th.

Under accrual accounting: The $15,000 revenue is recorded in February — when the work was completed and earned.

The journal entries:

February (revenue earned):

Debit: Accounts Receivable$15,000
Credit: Consulting Revenue$15,000

April (payment received):

Debit: Cash$15,000
Credit: Accounts Receivable$15,000

Expense Matching Example

That same consulting firm hired a subcontractor for $5,000 to help with the February project, but doesn't pay them until March.

Under accrual accounting: The $5,000 expense is recorded in February — matching it with the revenue it helped generate.

Debit: Subcontractor Expense$5,000
Credit: Accounts Payable$5,000

Now February's P&L shows the complete picture: $15,000 revenue - $5,000 expense = $10,000 profit. Accurate, meaningful, actionable.

Types of Accrual Entries

There are four main types of accrual adjustments that bookkeepers handle at month-end:

1. Accrued Revenue (Earned, Not Yet Billed)

Revenue earned but not yet invoiced. Common in professional services where work happens before billing.

2. Accrued Expenses (Incurred, Not Yet Paid)

Expenses incurred but not yet paid or recorded. Examples: wages earned by employees but not yet paid, interest on loans, utilities used.

3. Deferred Revenue (Received, Not Yet Earned)

Cash received for services not yet delivered. Common in SaaS, subscriptions, and retainer-based businesses. The cash goes to a liability account (Deferred Revenue) and is recognized as revenue when earned.

4. Prepaid Expenses (Paid, Not Yet Used)

Expenses paid in advance. Annual insurance premiums, prepaid rent, software subscriptions. Recorded as an asset, then expensed over the period they benefit.

Why Accrual Accounting Matters for Growing Businesses

  • Accurate profitability: Shows true profit by matching revenue with related expenses
  • Better decision-making: Monthly P&L reflects actual business performance, not just cash timing
  • Investor/lender ready: GAAP-compliant financials are required for fundraising and lending
  • Tax planning: Predictable revenue and expense recognition enables better tax strategies
  • Meaningful metrics: KPIs like gross margin, DSO, and operating leverage only work with accrual data
  • Audit compliance: Required for companies over $25M revenue and all public companies

The Monthly Close Process Under Accrual Accounting

Accrual accounting requires a disciplined monthly close process:

  1. Record all transactions — Ensure every invoice, bill, and payment is entered
  2. Reconcile bank accounts — Match books to bank statements
  3. Record accrual entries — Revenue accruals, expense accruals, prepaid amortization
  4. Record depreciation — Monthly depreciation on fixed assets
  5. Review accounts receivable — Age analysis, bad debt provisions
  6. Review accounts payable — Ensure all vendor bills are captured
  7. Reconcile payroll — Match payroll entries to payroll reports
  8. Generate financial statements — P&L, balance sheet, cash flow statement
  9. Review and analyze — Compare to budget, prior period, identify trends

Common Accrual Accounting Mistakes

  • Not reversing prior-period accruals: This creates double-counted expenses
  • Ignoring deferred revenue: Recognizing all cash received as revenue inflates income
  • Forgetting prepaid amortization: Annual payments need to be spread across 12 months
  • Inconsistent cut-off dates: Revenue and expenses must use the same period boundaries
  • Skipping the cash flow statement: Accrual P&L doesn't show cash — you need a separate cash flow report
