Profit and Loss Statement: Complete Guide with Examples

How to create, read, and analyze P&L statements — the foundation of financial advisory

The profit and loss statement (P&L) — also known as the income statement — is the single most important financial report for understanding whether a business is making or losing money. For bookkeepers transitioning into advisory roles, being able to not just prepare but analyze and explain a P&L is what separates a $35/hour bookkeeper from a $200/hour advisor.

What Is a Profit and Loss Statement?

A P&L statement summarizes all revenue, costs, and expenses during a specific period — typically monthly, quarterly, or annually. The bottom line tells you whether the business earned a net profit or suffered a net loss.

The P&L Formula:
Revenue − Cost of Goods Sold = Gross Profit
Gross Profit − Operating Expenses = Operating Income
Operating Income − Other Expenses + Other Income = Net Profit (or Loss)

Key P&L Metrics Every Advisor Must Know

1. Gross Margin

Formula: (Revenue − COGS) ÷ Revenue × 100

Measures how efficiently the business delivers its core service/product. For service businesses, aim for 60-80%. Below 50% signals pricing or delivery problems.

2. Operating Margin

Formula: Operating Income ÷ Revenue × 100

Shows profitability after all operating costs. A healthy service business targets 15-25%. This is the metric that tells you if the business model works.

3. Net Margin

Formula: Net Profit ÷ Revenue × 100

The final bottom line after everything — including taxes and interest. Above 10% is generally strong for small businesses.

4. Revenue Growth Rate

Formula: (Current Period Revenue − Prior Period Revenue) ÷ Prior Period Revenue × 100

Compare month-over-month and year-over-year. Consistent growth above 10% annually is excellent.

How to Analyze a P&L for Advisory Clients

This is where bookkeepers become advisors — and where the real money is. Don't just hand clients a P&L. Walk them through insights:

  1. Trend analysis: Compare 3-6 months side by side. Are margins improving or declining?
  2. Expense ratios: What percentage of revenue goes to each expense category? Flag anything that's growing faster than revenue.
  3. Revenue concentration: Is 80% of revenue coming from one client or one service? That's a risk.
  4. Seasonality: Identify cyclical patterns and help clients plan cash reserves for slow months.
  5. Benchmarking: Compare to industry averages. If competitors run at 25% net margin and your client is at 12%, there's room to optimize.
Advisory Gold: The most valuable thing you can do with a P&L is not report what happened — it's explain why it happened and what the client should do about it. That's the difference between a $35/hour and a $200/hour professional.

Common P&L Mistakes

  • Mixing personal and business expenses: Especially common in small businesses
  • Wrong revenue recognition: Recording revenue when invoiced vs. when earned — learn more about revenue recognition
  • Inconsistent categorization: Same expense type in different categories month to month
  • Missing accruals: Expenses incurred but not yet billed — common with accrual accounting
  • Ignoring owner compensation: Owner-operators often don't pay themselves a salary, distorting true profitability

P&L for Different Industries

Service Businesses

No inventory or traditional COGS. Cost of revenue is primarily contractor/labor costs. Expect gross margins of 60-80%.

Retail & E-commerce

COGS is the largest expense (product cost, shipping). Gross margins typically 30-60%. Watch inventory write-downs.

Construction

Uses percentage-of-completion or completed-contract method. Construction P&L requires matching revenue to project progress.

SaaS / Technology

Very high gross margins (70-90%) but heavy operating expenses in R&D and sales. Watch customer acquisition cost vs. lifetime value.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a profit and loss statement?

A profit and loss statement (P&L), also called an income statement, summarizes a company's revenues, costs, and expenses over a specific period, showing whether the business made or lost money.

What is the difference between a P&L and a balance sheet?

A P&L shows performance over a period (revenues minus expenses = profit), while a balance sheet shows financial position at a point in time (assets = liabilities + equity). The P&L feeds into retained earnings on the balance sheet.

How often should a P&L be prepared?

Most businesses prepare P&L statements monthly for internal management, quarterly for stakeholders, and annually for tax filing. Advisory professionals recommend monthly P&L reviews with clients to catch issues early.
