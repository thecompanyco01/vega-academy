What Is a Profit and Loss Statement?

A profit and loss statement (also called an income statement or P&L) summarizes a business's revenues, costs, and expenses over a specific period — monthly, quarterly, or annually. It answers the most fundamental business question: did we make money or lose money?

While a balance sheet shows what a business owns and owes at a single point in time, the P&L shows the flow of money in and out over a period. Together with the cash flow statement, these three reports form the foundation of financial analysis.

The Five Key Lines on Every P&L

1. Revenue (Top Line)

Total income from sales of products or services before any deductions. This is your "top line" — the starting point. Revenue growth is important, but it means nothing without profitability. A business can grow revenue 50% while losing money on every sale.

2. Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)

Direct costs of producing your product or delivering your service. For a manufacturer: raw materials, direct labor, factory overhead. For a service business: direct labor hours, subcontractor costs, project-specific expenses.

Advisory insight: COGS is where most profitability problems hide. A 5% reduction in COGS drops straight to the bottom line. If your client has $1M in COGS, a 5% reduction adds $50K in profit.

3. Gross Profit & Gross Margin

Gross Profit = Revenue – COGS. Gross Margin = Gross Profit ÷ Revenue × 100.

This tells you how efficiently the business converts revenue into profit before operating expenses. Industry benchmarks matter here:

IndustryTypical Gross Margin
Software/SaaS70–85%
Professional Services50–70%
Retail25–50%
Restaurants60–70%
Manufacturing25–45%
Construction15–30%

4. Operating Expenses (OpEx)

All costs of running the business that aren't directly tied to production: salaries, rent, marketing, technology, insurance, professional services. These are your "fixed" costs — they don't fluctuate much with sales volume.

Advisory insight: The ratio of OpEx to Revenue reveals operational efficiency. If OpEx is growing faster than revenue, the business has a structural problem.

5. Net Income (Bottom Line)

The final profit after all expenses, interest, and taxes. This is what the business actually earned. A positive bottom line means the business is profitable. A negative one means it's burning cash.

How to Analyze a Profit and Loss Statement

Vertical Analysis (Common-Size)

Express every line item as a percentage of revenue. This makes it easy to compare across periods and against industry benchmarks, regardless of company size.

Horizontal Analysis (Trend)

Compare the same line items across multiple periods to identify trends. Is revenue growing? Are margins expanding or contracting? Which expense categories are increasing fastest?

Ratio Analysis

Key P&L ratios every advisor should calculate monthly:

  • Gross margin: Is the business producing efficiently?
  • Operating margin: Is the business running efficiently?
  • Net profit margin: What's the actual return on revenue?
  • Revenue per employee: How productive is the team?
  • Marketing as % of revenue: Is customer acquisition sustainable?

Variance Analysis

Compare actual results to budget or forecast. Where are the biggest variances? What caused them? This is the heart of monthly advisory meetings.

Common P&L Red Flags Every Advisor Should Spot

  1. Declining gross margin: Costs are rising faster than prices. Either pricing needs to increase or production efficiency needs improvement.
  2. Revenue up, profit down: The business is growing unprofitably — likely underpricing or losing control of costs.
  3. One-time items masking trends: A large one-time gain can hide declining operations. Strip these out for true performance.
  4. OpEx growing faster than revenue: The business is becoming less efficient as it grows. Structural issue.
  5. Heavy concentration: If 50%+ of revenue comes from one customer or product, the business has dangerous concentration risk.

P&L Statement by Business Type

Service Businesses

Revenue = fees billed. COGS = direct labor + subcontractors. The key metric is effective hourly rate: total revenue ÷ total billable hours. Many service businesses discover their effective rate is far below their posted rate once non-billable time is factored in.

Product Businesses

Revenue = units sold × price. COGS = materials + direct labor + manufacturing overhead. Key metrics: gross margin per unit, contribution margin, break-even volume.

SaaS / Subscription Businesses

Revenue = MRR × months. COGS = hosting + support + onboarding. Key metrics: gross margin (target 70%+), CAC payback period, LTV:CAC ratio, net revenue retention.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between a profit and loss statement and an income statement?

They're the same thing. "Profit and loss statement" (P&L) is the common business term. "Income statement" is the formal accounting term. Same report, different names.

How often should a business review its P&L?

Monthly at minimum. Weekly is better for high-growth or cash-constrained businesses. The faster you spot trends, the faster you can act on them.

What's a good net profit margin for a small business?

It varies enormously by industry. Service businesses typically aim for 15–25%. Retail and restaurants: 3–9%. Software: 20–40%. The key is comparing against your specific industry benchmarks, not a generic "good" number.

Can I use the P&L to predict cash flow?

Not directly — the P&L includes non-cash items (depreciation) and doesn't capture timing of cash inflows/outflows (accounts receivable delays, inventory purchases). You need both the P&L and cash flow statement for a complete picture.
